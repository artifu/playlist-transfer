Why songs go missing during a transfer.

A missing result does not always mean the song is absent from Apple Music. It means PlaylistXfer could not find a candidate that was safe enough to select automatically.

PlaylistXfer editorial · Last reviewed July 22, 2026

Missing is a safety decision

When catalog evidence is weak or contradictory, PlaylistXfer leaves the track out. That is more honest than filling the playlist with a live take, cover, clean edit, or unrelated recording.

Six common reasons a track is not ready

1
Local files

Spotify can play audio stored on your own device. That file is not a Spotify catalog recording, so a catalog-to-catalog transfer cannot copy it into Apple Music automatically.

2
Different regional availability

Music rights vary by country. A recording available to your Spotify account may not appear in the Apple Music storefront used for the destination account.

3
A release was replaced

Labels sometimes remove an album and publish a new catalog edition. The same song may return with a different catalog ID, album title, artwork, or date.

4
Only another version is available

Apple Music may have a remaster, compilation appearance, clean edit, or live performance while the exact Spotify edition is unavailable.

5
Metadata is incomplete or ambiguous

Generic titles, inconsistent featured-artist credits, and missing recording identifiers can produce several equally plausible candidates.

6
The item is not a standard music track

Podcast episodes, audiobooks, video items, and other Spotify-specific media are not equivalent to Apple Music catalog songs.

First, read the status correctly

Ready

A strong Apple Music candidate exists and can be included without another decision.

Review

One or more plausible candidates exist, but the version or metadata needs your judgment.

Missing

No candidate crossed the current confidence threshold. Nothing is substituted silently.

Review and missing are different. A review row gives you a candidate to inspect. A missing row says the catalog search did not produce a safe choice under the current conditions.

When another edition may be acceptable

Available candidate Main risk What to verify
Compilation appearance Different album artwork and date Artist, ISRC when available, and duration
Remastered release Different mastering Remaster year, edition label, and runtime
Clean edit Lyrics differ from the source Content rating and title suffix
Radio edit Shortened arrangement Duration and release name
Live version Different performance Venue, “live” label, album, and duration

A safe recovery workflow

  1. Open the source track in Spotify. Confirm its complete title, artist credit, album, and version markers.
  2. Search Apple Music manually. Try the artist plus the core title, then inspect albums and alternate editions instead of choosing the first result.
  3. Compare duration and version labels. A close duration supports a match; a large difference deserves caution.
  4. Choose based on your playlist’s purpose. A compilation copy may be fine for casual listening, while a carefully sequenced album playlist may need the exact edition.
  5. Skip if the evidence is weak. Finish the transfer, then add the correct track manually if it becomes available.
Do not chase a 100% result.

A smaller accurate playlist is easier to repair than a complete-looking playlist containing wrong versions that go unnoticed.

Items that need a different workflow

Local files cannot become Apple Music catalog entries through PlaylistXfer. If you own the audio file, use Apple’s supported library or sync workflow separately. Podcasts and other non-song items also require their destination service rather than an Apple Music playlist.

PlaylistXfer transfers catalog references, not audio. It never downloads a Spotify track and uploads it elsewhere.
